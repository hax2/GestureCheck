GMSM Interpretation

Preliminary read of the Flash and Pro gesture ratings

What the ratings suggest

The VLMs produced largely consistent zero-shot estimates of Gesture Memory Support Model dimensions directly from gesture videos. Agreement was strongest for visually observable and motorically grounded properties, while disagreement increased for dimensions that require more semantic or cultural interpretation.

The important signal is that disagreement was systematic rather than random. Abstract, metaphorical, arbitrary, or semantically ambiguous gestures produced greater variability, while concrete words and highly iconic gestures produced stronger model agreement.

Strongest agreement

  • Enactment potential
  • Gesture complexity fit
  • Concrete, visually transparent gestures
  • Actions with clear motor grounding

Higher disagreement

  • Iconicity when the mapping is metaphoric
  • Sensorimotor imagery for weakly grounded items
  • Cultural familiarity for arbitrary gestures
  • Abstract or semantically ambiguous vocabulary

Why this matters

For second language vocabulary learning research, the results support the feasibility of automated gesture annotation as a scalable way to estimate theoretically meaningful gesture-based covariates. This is especially useful in cognition and cognitive neuroscience, where such annotations are costly, slow, and often missing.

The rationales are as important as the scores. The models did not appear to only detect motion patterns; they generated coherent multimodal explanations for why a gesture was more iconic, more enactable, more culturally familiar, or more ambiguous.

Theoretical fit

Concrete vocabulary items were interpreted more consistently than abstract lexical items, which fits embodied cognition accounts: concrete concepts are more directly grounded in perceptual and motor representations. This also matches the behavioral pattern from gesture-based learning experiments, where concrete words and iconic gestures tend to be strongest.

Limits

Culturally dependent, weakly grounded, metaphoric, and arbitrary gestures remain difficult. In teaching contexts these gestures can be explained, but in experimental contexts participants may need time to learn the gesture-word mapping. The model variability on these items is therefore theoretically informative, not just noise.
